Deep beneath the ocean surface, tectonic plates engage in a perpetual, unhurried migration across the planet's mantle. At divergent boundaries, the rocky crust fractures, permitting superheated magma to ascend from the volatile interior. This molten material solidifies upon contact with frigid seawater, forging new geological ridges along the seafloor. At convergent zones, one dense plate is inevitably forced beneath another, sinking into the subterranean depths where it melts. These forces generate tremendous friction, periodically releasing built-up kinetic energy as devastating seismic tremors. Through this cyclical process of creation and destruction, the topography is constantly remodeled over millions of years.
