The ancient art of glassblowing begins in the high heat of a specialized furnace, where raw silica sand is melted into a glowing, viscous liquid. A skilled artisan carefully gathers a precise portion of this molten glass onto the end of a long, hollow steel blowpipe. By continuously rotating the heavy pipe, the maker prevents the fluid material from succumbing to gravity and dripping. The artisan introduces a short burst of air into the pipe, creating a hollow bubble within the center of the molten mass. Using wet wooden blocks, thick metal jacks, and damp newspaper, the exterior is methodically contoured. Before the fragile piece cools and shatters from thermal shock, it is moved to an annealing oven to lower its temperature gradually.
