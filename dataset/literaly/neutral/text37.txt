The formation of coastal fog relies on a specific convergence of atmospheric conditions and deep ocean currents. During the warmer months, the sun heats the inland valleys, causing air masses to rise and creating an area of low pressure. Simultaneously, cold water from the deep ocean is brought to the surface near the coastline through the process of upwelling. Moist, warm air from the open sea blows across this frigid coastal water, plunging its temperature to the dew point. The invisible vapor rapidly condenses into microscopic droplets, forming a thick, low-lying cloud bank. Drawn inland by the pressure gradient, this marine layer blankets the coastal hills in a heavy, cooling mist until the afternoon sun burns it away.
