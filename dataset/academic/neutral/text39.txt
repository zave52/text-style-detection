The systematic cataloging of newly discovered exoplanetary bodies heavily relies upon the precise interpretation of minute fluctuations in stellar luminosity. Astrophysicists utilize orbital telescopes to continuously monitor targeted star systems for prolonged periods, measuring the exact percentage of light blocked during a planetary transit event. These complex astronomical observations require the implementation of advanced algorithmic filters to eliminate ambient cosmic noise and isolate the genuine planetary signal. This peer-reviewed research paper provides a comprehensive overview of the mathematical techniques employed by space agencies to accurately determine the mass, orbital velocity, and approximate atmospheric composition of these distant celestial objects.
