The primary objective of this experimental investigation is to accurately measure the structural integrity and thermal resistance of newly synthesized polymer composites under extreme atmospheric pressure conditions. Previous studies within the field of materials science have extensively documented the baseline tensile strength of traditional carbon-fiber reinforced plastics. However, there remains a notable lack of comprehensive empirical data concerning the long-term degradation rates of these specific advanced materials when subjected to continuous thermal cycling. To address this significant gap in the existing literature, a series of controlled laboratory stress tests were systematically conducted using highly calibrated mechanical testing apparatuses. The composite samples were exposed to incremental temperature variations ranging from standard ambient room conditions to absolute thermal thresholds established by earlier theoretical models. The quantitative results were continuously logged and digitally analyzed using standard statistical software to generate precise failure probability curves. The final data demonstrates a clear, measurable correlation between prolonged thermal exposure and the gradual deterioration of the internal polymer matrix structure.
